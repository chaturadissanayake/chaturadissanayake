Exploration

Where the exploration started

The instinct was to reach for what Sri Lanka signals most naturally — sun, warmth, tropical abundance. The first direction followed that instinct. It took three rounds of critique to understand why it was wrong.

Exploration began on paper, with 20+ thumbnail directions generated before any digital work. The goal was to exhaust the obvious answers quickly — tourism aesthetics, cliché heritage motifs, generic premium signals — so the less obvious ones could emerge.

Direction explored

Solar motif system

The first direction used radiant sun forms to evoke tropical origin, growth cycles, and the equatorial climate that makes Ceylon cinnamon distinct. The forms were warm, structured, and immediately legible as Sri Lankan in character.

Why we moved on

It read as tourism, not trade

Solar iconography — however refined — carries strong associations with the Sri Lankan tourism sector. For buyers in European delis and wellness retailers, the visual signal was wrong. The brand needed to communicate supply-chain expertise and traceability, not destination. The pivot to botanical illustration grounded every visual claim in the actual product.

Design decision — the botanical seal

Every element within the seal is a real product. Every element is traceable to a real place of origin. The complexity is earned — not decorative.

Botanical illustration has a long history in scientific publishing — Linnaean taxonomy, colonial-era herbaria, pharmaceutical monographs. These are documents of expertise, not aspiration. That register was the right visual language for a brand built on traceability and provenance. The mark functions as a credential: auditors, trade buyers, and end consumers can each read it differently and find something true in it.

Typographic reasoning

Baskerville Old Face carries the implied authority of centuries of print. Poppins carries the functional clarity modern trade buyers need to act on.

Six typeface pairings were tested against the brief before this combination was selected. The criteria were specific: the display face had to read as knowledgeable rather than nostalgic; the body face had to feel modern without erasing the heritage context. The pair was chosen not for convention but for the specific tension the brand needs to hold — ancient and tradeable, rooted and exportable.

08 — Outcome & reflection

3+ Visual directions explored before arriving at the botanical seal system
12 Individual ingredient illustrations, each traceable to a Sri Lankan growing region
6 Typeface pairings tested against the brief before Baskerville + Poppins was selected

This project clarified a question I now apply to all provenance-driven branding work: does this visual choice demonstrate expertise, or does it borrow aesthetic authority from somewhere else? The solar motif direction was visually strong — but it borrowed from tourism, not trade. The botanical seal earned its authority by making every element accountable to a real product. That discipline — complexity that is earned rather than applied — has become a principle I carry into subsequent projects.

The challengeCeylon cinnamon is among the most coveted spices in the world — and among the most misrepresented. In Western markets it lives in the shadow of generic "cinnamon," often cassia, sold at a fraction of its actual value. The brief was to build a brand that could correct this: establish authentic provenance, command premium price points, and earn the trust of trade buyers who are professionally sceptical of origin claims.

The insightSri Lankan export branding defaults to the postcard — the elephant, the sunrise, the golden coast. These signals work for tourism. They actively undermine credibility in premium food and wellness retail, where buyers need to believe in supply-chain expertise and traceability. Heritage authority and heritage cliché look identical from the wrong angle.

The approachThe identity had to demonstrate knowledge, not declare it. The botanical seal was the structural answer: every element is a real product, traceable to a specific region of Sri Lankan soil. There is no decorative filler — the complexity of the mark is earned by the breadth of what the land genuinely produces. The mark functions as a credential.

Typography & colourBaskerville Old Face was selected as the primary typeface after testing six pairings against the brief. It carries the implied authority of scientific and legal print traditions without reading as nostalgic. Poppins provides the functional clarity modern trade buyers need to act efficiently. The colour palette — sage, deep forest, warm parchment — was drawn from the ingredients themselves, not from category convention.
